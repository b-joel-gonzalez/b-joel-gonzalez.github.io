Research

I spent a significant part of my undergraduate years narrowing down my research interests, working in predominantly software engineering positions. I worked in three labs at UPenn during those years - one in Physics, one in Computer Graphics, and one in Robotics. They were valuable experiences that helped me learn about what topics I enjoyed and how to become a better researcher - check out my CV if you'd like to learn about what I did in each lab.

As a graduate student, however, I've become much more interested in hardware work, particularly involving electronics. To this end, I've chosen to focus on an electronics project for my master's thesis this year, to be completed in May 2021. For this project, I am working with two companies at Pennovation (Instahub and Automation Research Group) in order to develop a hybrid sensor device for occupancy detection. Our goal for this project is to look into a variety of sensors - from mm-wave to PIR to image - and combine them in order to develop an efficient device that accurately detects a human occupant. I will link my thesis here once it's completed!

Through this work, I've found a deep interest in electronics engineering. I want to work to develop more efficient electronics, which push the boundaries of today's computational power. I'm interested in working across all disciplines of electronics, from digital to analog to power, and utilizing a multitude of technologies, from mm-wave to RF and more. I hope to eventually pursue a Ph.D. in electrical engineering in order to conduct novel research and to help expand the field.
